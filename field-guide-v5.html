The canvas was never the design. Whether you're in Claude Design, Figma Make, Stitch, Canva, or whatever ships next — the conversation happens before anything appears on screen. The design happens in the conversation. Everything after is rendering.

This field guide works with any AI design tool. Not prompts. Not templates. Ways to work — the moves and disciplines that separate design thinking in conversation from design-shaped output.

Run this first  ·  Five questions in the Eames tradition

Five Questions. Ten Minutes.

Answer these before you open any design tool. The thinking that happens here is the design.

01
What is the purpose?
Not "make a dashboard." What specific outcome does this achieve? For whom? In what moment of their day?
02
Who, exactly?
Name a real person. Not "users." The person implies the aesthetic. The aesthetic does not imply the person.
03
What are you working within?
Constraints are not obstacles. They are the shape of the solution.
04
What is the one thing?
Three seconds. One thing to understand, feel, or do. Not three. One.
05
How will you know it worked?
Not "it looks good." What changes in behaviour, decision, or understanding?

Cluster 01 · 8 ways
Before You Start
The design happens here. Before you open anything.
  • 01
    Answer "what is this for?" before you describe what it looks like.
    Purpose precedes form. Every time. If you can't say it in one sentence, you're not ready to design.
  • 02
    Name a real person before you name a colour.
    "A CFO who makes decisions in the margins of other meetings" is more useful than "professional, clean, corporate." The person implies the aesthetic. The aesthetic does not imply the person.
  • 03
    Write down your constraints before your preferences.
    Brand system, technical environment, time, approvals. State these first. They are not limiting, they are generative. They're what makes this solution different from every other solution.
  • 04
    Separate what you know from what you're assuming.
    Before you prompt, list three things you know about this user and three things you're assuming. The assumptions are where the design will go wrong.
  • 05
    Define success in human behaviour, not aesthetics.
    "The user understands the hierarchy immediately" is a success criterion. "It looks clean" is not.
  • 06
    Ask: who is NOT the audience?
    Exclusion sharpens intent. A tool designed for everyone is designed for no one. Naming who this is not for clarifies what it does not need to be.
  • 07
    Read your brand guide before your first prompt, not after.
    AI will apply your brand faithfully and incorrectly if you brief it from memory. Know what you're actually asking it to apply.
  • 08
    Run the five questions.
    Purpose, person, constraints, one thing, success measure. Five questions in the Eames tradition. Ten minutes. See above.

Cluster 02 · 13 ways
Prompting for Intent
You have the intent. Here's how to get it out of your head and into language precise enough to produce something real.
  • 09
    Lead with purpose, not description.
    "This needs to help a CFO make a fast decision under time pressure" is better than "make a clean executive dashboard." Purpose tells the AI what to optimise for. Description tells it what to copy.
  • 10
    Give context about the moment of use.
    "Someone reading this on their phone between meetings" changes the design. "Someone presenting this in a boardroom" changes it differently. The moment of use is design information.
  • 11
    Specify what the user needs to understand, feel, or do — not what they need to see.
    Outcome before output. "They need to leave knowing what action to take" is more useful than "it should have a clear call to action."
  • 12
    Include what you're NOT trying to do.
    Negative space in your brief is as important as positive. "This should not feel like a sales document" removes a whole category of wrong answers before you've seen a single output.
  • 13
    State your constraints as facts, not apologies.
    "This needs to work in our existing blue and white system" not "sorry but we need to use our brand colours." Constraints are design information. Apologising for them obscures that.
  • 14
    Give the AI a role.
    "Approach this as a UX designer who has read the user research" produces different work than "approach this as a graphic designer." The role implies a hierarchy of values.
  • 15
    Reference qualities, not styles.
    "The clarity of a well-designed government service, the warmth of a trusted healthcare brand, but neither's visual language" tells the AI what qualities you want, not what to copy.
  • 16
    Specify the emotional register.
    "Confident but not aggressive." "Approachable but authoritative." Designers have always specified emotional register. Now you do it in language rather than on a moodboard.
  • 17
    Tell the AI what you know about your user's expertise level.
    A design for experts can assume vocabulary, compress hierarchy, and skip explanation. A design for newcomers cannot. This changes almost everything about the output.
  • 18
    Give the AI your failure mode.
    "The last version looked beautiful but nobody understood the hierarchy" is gold. Failure modes tell the tool what success actually requires. Use them.
  • 19
    Include the business context.
    Who commissioned this. What is at stake. What the approval process looks like. These constraints shape good design as much as brand guidelines do.
  • 20
    Prompt for multiple directions before you commit to one.
    Ask the AI to generate three distinct directions before you iterate on any of them. Premature commitment is the most common mistake in AI-assisted design.
  • 21
    Ask for the reasoning behind the first version.
    Before you react to what the AI generated, ask why it made the choices it made. You'll often discover that what you thought you wanted is not what you actually need.

Cluster 03 · 10 ways
Iterating Through Conversation
How to refine without losing ground. How to know when you're done.
  • 22
    Separate "I don't understand it" from "I don't like it."
    Not understanding is a clarity problem. Not liking it is a values or taste problem. Know which one you have before you prompt again.
  • 23
    When something works, name it explicitly before you ask for changes.
    "Keep the hierarchy in the header section exactly as it is." What you don't protect, you lose.
  • 24
    Be specific about what is wrong.
    "The hierarchy feels off" is less useful than "the user's eye goes to the data table before the decision prompt, and it needs to be the other way around."
  • 25
    Don't start over when you can redirect.
    "Keep the structure, change the emotional register" preserves what is working. Starting over resets everything, including the things that were right.
  • 26
    Use the tool's custom controls as vocabulary.
    The sliders and controls your AI tool generates show you which parameters it understands. Use them to understand your own brief better.
  • 27
    Ask the AI to explain its decisions before you critique them.
    "Walk me through why you made these layout choices" surfaces assumptions the AI made that you didn't intend.
  • 28
    When you're stuck, change the constraint rather than the aesthetic.
    If you've been iterating on how something looks without progress, you probably have a constraint problem, not an aesthetic problem. Go back to the brief.
  • 29
    Restate the original purpose periodically.
    Drift is real in long design conversations. Every five or six exchanges, restate the one-sentence purpose. It recalibrates both you and the AI.
  • 30
    Treat unexpected outputs as data.
    If the AI went somewhere surprising, ask why before you dismiss it. Unexpected directions often reveal that your brief was ambiguous, which is useful to know.
  • 31
    Know when to stop iterating.
    Conversation without commitment is not design. When the output is good enough to test, test it. Perfection through iteration is a trap.

Cluster 04 · 8 ways
Critique and Evaluation
You've seen the output. Now look at it like a designer.
  • 32
    Evaluate against purpose first, aesthetics second.
    Does this accomplish what it needs to accomplish? Aesthetics serve purpose. They don't replace it.
  • 33
    Ask: would the real user understand this in three seconds?
    Not a designer. Not your team. The person you named at the beginning. In the moment of use you described. Three seconds.
  • 34
    Test the hierarchy before the colour.
    Does the eye go where it needs to go? Hierarchy is the structure of meaning. Colour is its decoration.
  • 35
    Ask the AI to critique its own output against your stated intent.
    "Given the brief I gave you, what are the weakest aspects of what you produced?" It surfaces gaps you missed and keeps you honest about whether you briefed it well.
  • 36
    Bring a second voice in before you export.
    Ask a colleague what they think the design is trying to do, without telling them. If their answer matches yours, the intent is landing.
  • 37
    Check that brand application is correct, not just present.
    AI will apply your brand faithfully and wrong. "On brand" and "correctly applying the brand" are not the same thing.
  • 38
    Evaluate for what is missing, not just what is there.
    What did you ask for that didn't appear? What assumption did the AI make that you didn't intend? Absence is as meaningful as presence.
  • 39
    Ask: does this solve the problem, or does it look like it solves the problem?
    One holds up in user testing. One holds up in approval meetings. Know which one you have.

Cluster 05 · 7 ways
Handoff and Output
The design intent needs to travel. Here's how to make sure it does.
  • 40
    Document the design intent before you export.
    Not what it looks like. Why it is that way. The decisions, the trade-offs, the constraints that shaped them. This is the information that travels with the work.
  • 41
    Export for collaboration, not just for editing.
    Canva, Figma, wherever your team works — that's where the design becomes a team object. Bring the intent documentation with it. A design without its rationale is a picture.
  • 42
    When handing off to a developer or build tool, include the design rationale, not just the spec.
    "This spacing creates visual breathing room for a user processing complex information" is more useful to an engineer than "padding: 32px." Rationale survives implementation. Spec often does not.
  • 43
    Keep the conversation thread.
    It is the design history. The decisions made, the directions rejected, the constraints that shaped the outcome. It is more valuable than the export.
  • 44
    Note which decisions were intentional and which were the AI's defaults.
    Defaults are not decisions. They may be right, but you should know the difference.
  • 45
    Brief the human who continues this work on the intent, not the output.
    "Here is what it is trying to do, for whom, in what moment, and how we will know it worked" is a brief. Give the brief.
  • 46
    Archive the prompts that produced good work.
    A prompt that produced excellent output is a reusable design asset. Treat it like one. Your prompt library is your methodology made portable.
