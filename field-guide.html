How to Work With
Claude Design

By MC Dean · 46 ways across 5 clusters · MIT License

The canvas was never the design. Charles and Ray Eames knew this. Design is a plan for arranging elements to accomplish a purpose — not a rendering, not a deliverable, not a file.

Claude Design launched April 17, 2026. You talk to it. It designs. Which means the design happens in the conversation. Everything after is rendering. This is the field guide that didn't exist yet.

The Eames Test

01
What is the purpose?
Not "make a dashboard." What specific outcome does this achieve? For whom? In what moment?
02
Who, exactly?
Name a real person if you can. Not "users." The busier and more specific, the better.
03
What are you working within?
Brand, time, technology, budget. Constraints are not obstacles — they are the shape of the solution.
04
What is the one thing?
If someone looked for three seconds, what single thing should they understand, feel, or do?
05
How will you know it worked?
Not "it looks good." What changes in behaviour, decision, or understanding?
Cluster 01 · 8 ways
Before You Start
The intent-setting moves. The design happens here, before you've opened anything.
  • 01
    Answer "what is this for?" before you describe what it looks like.
    Purpose precedes form. Every time. If you cannot answer this in one sentence, you are not ready to design.
  • 02
    Name a real person before you name a colour.
    "A CFO who makes decisions in the margins of other meetings" is more useful than "professional, clean, corporate." The person implies the aesthetic. The aesthetic does not imply the person.
  • 03
    Write down your constraints before your preferences.
    Constraints are the design. Brand system, technical environment, time, approvals. State these first. They are not limiting — they are generative. They are what makes this solution different from every other solution.
  • 04
    Separate what you know from what you are assuming.
    Before you prompt, list three things you know about this user and three things you are assuming. The assumptions are where the design will go wrong.
  • 05
    Define success in human behaviour, not aesthetics.
    "The user understands the hierarchy immediately" is a success criterion. "It looks clean" is not. Describe what a successful outcome looks like in terms of what a person does, decides, or understands differently.
  • 06
    Ask: who is NOT the audience?
    Exclusion sharpens intent. A tool designed for everyone is designed for no one. Naming who this is not for clarifies what it does not need to be.
  • 07
    Read your brand guide before your first prompt, not after.
    AI will apply your brand faithfully and incorrectly if you brief it from memory. Know what you are actually asking it to apply.
  • 08
    Run the Eames Test.
    Purpose, person, constraints, one thing, success measure. Five questions. Ten minutes. See above.
Cluster 02 · 13 ways
Prompting for Intent
How to articulate what you are designing precisely enough to produce useful outputs.
  • 09
    Lead with purpose, not description.
    "This needs to help a CFO make a fast decision under time pressure" is better than "make a clean executive dashboard." Purpose tells Claude what to optimise for. Description tells it what to copy.
  • 10
    Give context about the moment of use.
    "Someone reading this on their phone between meetings" changes the design. "Someone presenting this in a boardroom to people who haven't seen it before" changes it differently. The moment of use is design information.
  • 11
    Specify what the user needs to understand, feel, or do — not what they need to see.
    Outcome before output. "They need to leave this knowing what action to take" is more useful than "it should have a clear call to action."
  • 12
    Include what you are NOT trying to do.
    Negative space in your brief is as important as positive. "This should not feel like a sales document" removes a whole category of wrong answers.
  • 13
    State your constraints as facts, not apologies.
    "This needs to work in our existing blue and white system" not "sorry but we need to use our brand colours." Constraints are design information. Apologising for them obscures that.
  • 14
    Give Claude a role.
    "Approach this as a UX designer who has read the user research" produces different work than "approach this as a graphic designer." The role implies a hierarchy of values.
  • 15
    Reference real examples for tone, not style.
    "The clarity of GOV.UK, the warmth of Airbnb, but neither's visual language" tells Claude what qualities you want, not what to copy. Quality references outperform style references.
  • 16
    Specify the emotional register.
    "Confident but not aggressive." "Approachable but authoritative." "Urgent but not panicked." Designers have always specified emotional register. Now you do it in language.
  • 17
    Tell Claude what you know about your user's expertise level.
    A design for experts can assume vocabulary, compress hierarchy, and skip explanation. A design for newcomers cannot. This changes almost everything about the output.
  • 18
    Give Claude your failure mode.
    "The last version looked beautiful but nobody understood the hierarchy" is gold. Failure modes tell Claude what success actually requires. Use them.
  • 19
    Include the business context.
    Who commissioned this. What is at stake. What the approval process looks like. These constraints shape good design as much as brand guidelines do.
  • 20
    Prompt for multiple directions before you commit.
    Ask Claude to generate three distinct directions before you iterate on any one of them. Premature commitment is the most common mistake in AI-assisted design.
  • 21
    Ask for the reasoning behind the first version.
    Before you react to what Claude generated, ask why it made the choices it made. You will learn whether your intent landed — and you will often discover that what you thought you wanted is not what you actually need.
Cluster 03 · 10 ways
Iterating Through Conversation
How to refine without losing ground. How to know when you are done.
  • 22
    Separate "I don't understand it" from "I don't like it."
    They need different responses. Not understanding is a clarity problem. Not liking it is a values or taste problem. Know which one you have before you prompt again.
  • 23
    When something works, name it explicitly.
    "Keep the hierarchy in the header section exactly as it is" before you ask for changes. What you do not protect, you lose.
  • 24
    Be specific about what is wrong.
    "The hierarchy feels off" is less useful than "the user's eye goes to the data table before the decision prompt, and it needs to be the other way around." Specific problems get specific solutions.
  • 25
    Don't start over when you can redirect.
    "Keep the structure, change the emotional register" preserves what is working. Starting over resets everything, including the things that were right.
  • 26
    Use Claude's sliders as vocabulary.
    The sliders Claude generates are showing you which parameters it understands about the design. They are the adjustable dimensions. Use them to understand your own brief better.
  • 27
    Ask Claude to explain its decisions before you critique them.
    "Walk me through why you made these layout choices" reveals whether your intent landed. It also surfaces assumptions Claude made that you did not intend.
  • 28
    When you are stuck, change the constraint rather than the aesthetic.
    If you have been iterating on how something looks without progress, you probably have a constraint problem, not an aesthetic problem. Go back to the brief.
  • 29
    Restate the original purpose periodically.
    Drift is real in long design conversations. Every five or six exchanges, restate the one-sentence purpose. It recalibrates both you and Claude.
  • 30
    Treat unexpected outputs as data.
    If Claude went somewhere surprising, ask why before you dismiss it. Unexpected directions often reveal that your brief was ambiguous — which is useful to know.
  • 31
    Know when to stop iterating.
    Conversation without commitment is not design. When the output is good enough to test, test it. Perfection through iteration is a trap.
Cluster 04 · 8 ways
Critique and Evaluation
How to assess what Claude generates against real design criteria.
  • 32
    Evaluate against purpose first, aesthetics second.
    Does this accomplish what it needs to accomplish? That is the only question that matters first. Aesthetics serve purpose — they do not replace it.
  • 33
    Ask: would the real user understand this in three seconds?
    Not a designer. Not your team. The person you named at the beginning. In the moment of use you described. Three seconds.
  • 34
    Test the hierarchy before the colour.
    Does the eye go where it needs to go? Does the most important thing read as most important? Hierarchy is the structure of meaning. Colour is its decoration.
  • 35
    Ask Claude to critique its own output against your stated intent.
    "Given the brief I gave you, what are the weakest aspects of what you produced?" It will surface gaps you missed. It also keeps you honest about whether you briefed it well.
  • 36
    Bring a second voice in before you export.
    Share your conversation with a colleague and ask them what they think the design is trying to do — without telling them. If their answer matches yours, the intent is landing.
  • 37
    Check that brand application is correct, not just present.
    AI will apply your brand faithfully and wrong. Check typography hierarchy, colour usage, spacing system. "On brand" and "correctly applying the brand" are not the same thing.
  • 38
    Evaluate for what is missing, not just what is there.
    What did you ask for that did not appear? What assumption did Claude make that you did not intend? Absence is as meaningful as presence in design critique.
  • 39
    Ask: does this solve the problem, or does it look like it solves the problem?
    One holds up in user testing. One holds up in approval meetings. Know which one you have.
Cluster 05 · 7 ways
Handoff and Output
Moving to Canva, Claude Code, or a human team without losing what the design knows.
  • 40
    Document the design intent before you export.
    Not what it looks like. Why it is that way. The decisions, the trade-offs, the constraints that shaped them. This is the information that travels with the work.
  • 41
    Export to Canva for collaboration, not just for editing.
    Canva is where the design becomes a team object. Bring the intent documentation with it. A design without its rationale is a picture.
  • 42
    When handing to Claude Code, include the design rationale, not just the spec.
    "This spacing creates visual breathing room for a user processing complex information" is more useful to an engineer than "padding: 32px." Rationale survives implementation. Spec often does not.
  • 43
    Keep the conversation thread.
    It is the design history. The decisions made, the directions rejected, the constraints that shaped the outcome. It is more valuable than the export.
  • 44
    Note which decisions were intentional and which were Claude's defaults.
    You want to know which aspects of the design were chosen and which were accepted. Defaults are not decisions. They may be right — but you should know the difference.
  • 45
    Brief the human who continues this work on the intent, not the output.
    "Here is what it looks like" is a handoff. "Here is what it is trying to do, for whom, in what moment, and how we will know it worked" is a brief. Give the brief.
  • 46
    Archive the prompts that produced good work.
    A prompt that produced excellent output is a reusable design asset. Treat it like one. Your prompt library is your methodology made portable.
